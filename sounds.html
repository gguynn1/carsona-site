Engine Sound Guide

How to import, name, and organize custom engine sounds in Carsona.

Three Sound Modes

Carsona plays engine audio driven by live OBD-II telemetry. You can choose from three modes in Settings → Engine Sound.

🎹 Synthesized

No audio files needed. The app generates engine harmonics in real time from RPM and throttle data. Cylinder count and timbre are configurable.

🎤 Classic RPM Packs

Best for realistic engine sounds and mechanical loops. Import WAV or MP3 files named with RPM anchors. The app crossfades between layers and pitch-shifts to track live RPM.

🎶 Sound Drive

Scene-based adaptive music engine. Multi-stem tracks (up to 8 roles) whose arrangement is driven by user-authored scenes — triggers based on speed, RPM, G-force, and other driving signals select which stems play. Transitions land on quantized beat or bar boundaries. No pitch-shifting; tempo stays locked.

Supported Formats

  • Import accepts .wav, .mp3, and .zip files.
  • A single .zip archive containing WAV/MP3 files is extracted automatically — handy for importing a complete pack in one step.
  • Imported files are normalized to WAV on device.
  • Files longer than 30 seconds are trimmed to the first 30 seconds.

Classic RPM Packs

Best for realistic engine sounds, mechanical loops, and anything that should get brighter or faster with RPM. Files that share a prefix become one pack.

Naming pattern

<prefix>_<rpm>rpm.wav
V8 muscle car
  • v8_750rpm.wav — burbling idle
  • v8_1500rpm.wav — low cruise
  • v8_3000rpm.wav — mid-range grunt
  • v8_5200rpm.wav — top-end roar
Sci-fi hoverbike
  • hoverbike_700rpm.wav — low electrical hum with faint turbine spin
  • hoverbike_1800rpm.wav — more rotor whine and pulse
  • hoverbike_3600rpm.wav — sharper plasma-like harmonics
  • hoverbike_5200rpm.wav — high-energy futuristic shriek
Diesel-industrial monster
  • diesel_beast_650rpm.wav — heavy chug and cabin vibration
  • diesel_beast_1400rpm.wav — turbo spool enters the texture
  • diesel_beast_2600rpm.wav — dense mechanical grind
  • diesel_beast_3800rpm.wav — angry boosted roar

How it works: the app finds the nearest lower and upper RPM files, crossfades between them, and varispeeds each sample to track live RPM. Two to five files per pack is usually enough — include idle, mid, and high RPM anchors.

Tips for RPM packs

  • Each file should sound like the engine at roughly that RPM.
  • Keep tonal character, background noise, and levels consistent.
  • Use clean, loopable recordings with no clicks at boundaries.
  • Avoid rhythmic content that must stay locked to a fixed tempo.
  • Minimal reverb and no long tails.

Sound Drive Packs

Best for music beds, rhythmic loops, drones, and any sound design that should stay tempo-stable instead of pitch-shifting with RPM. A scene engine controls which stems play together, and triggers based on driving telemetry select the active scene. Transitions land on quantized beat or bar boundaries for a musical feel.

Naming patterns

<prefix>_<role>.wav
<prefix>_<role>_<N>bpm.wav

If you use the BPM suffix, include it on every stem for clarity. All BPM declarations within a pack must agree — conflicting values will log a warning and the first declared BPM wins. If no file declares BPM, the pack defaults to 120 and you can override it in the scene editor.

Stem roles

Most packs need only the four core roles. The optional roles exist for producers who want finer-grained scene control. Roles are generic labels — they do not imply a fixed driving-signal mapping. Which stems play is determined by user-authored scenes.

Suffix Musical purpose
_bass Low-frequency energy layer Core
_melody Main harmonic or melodic layer Core
_drums Rhythmic backbone Core
_pad Sustained atmosphere Core
_lead Featured melodic accent Optional
_perc Percussive accents and fills Optional
_fx Effects and transition moments Optional
_texture Ambient bed or noisy texture Optional

Scene engine

After importing a Sound Drive pack, open the scene editor from Settings → Engine Sound → Sound Drive Pack → Edit Scenes. Each scene defines:

  • A name for the arrangement state.
  • Which stems are active (on/off toggles for every stem in the pack).
  • A trigger condition — a threshold comparison against a driving signal (speed, RPM, G-force, etc.), or compound AND/OR logic combining multiple conditions, or “always active” for the default scene.

Scenes are evaluated in priority order — the first matching scene wins. If no trigger matches, the default scene plays. New packs auto-generate intensity-based scenes that progressively add stems as driving gets more spirited — responding to speed, cornering, and overall driving dynamics. No manual setup needed.

  • cyberpunk_drums_120bpm.wav — tight electronic beat

  • cyberpunk_lead_120bpm.wav — acid synth stabs

Auto-generated scenes build intensity from speed, cornering, and spirited driving: “Chill” (bass + pad, default), “Cruising” (add drums when moving or turning), “Spirited” (add melody when driving hard or cornering), “Full Send” (everything at high speed or aggressive driving). Customize in Edit Scenes.

Canyon road orchestral
  • canyon_bass_90bpm.wav — deep cello ostinato
  • canyon_melody_90bpm.wav — strings and French horn
  • canyon_drums_90bpm.wav — timpani pulse and snare rolls
  • canyon_pad_90bpm.wav — choir and string sustain
  • canyon_perc_90bpm.wav — triangle and woodblock accents
  • canyon_fx_90bpm.wav — brass stabs and cymbal swells

How it works: all stems loop simultaneously at their original tempo. The scene engine evaluates telemetry triggers with hysteresis and debounce to prevent chatter, then applies scene changes on quantized beat or bar boundaries. No pitch-shifting is applied, so rhythmic content stays locked to tempo.

Tips for Sound Drive packs

  • Think in layers, not songs.
  • Design stems that sound complete in various combinations — scenes control which subset plays at any time.
  • Keep all files in the same key, tempo, and compatible tonal center.
  • Prefer textures, pads, percussion beds, and filtered arps over prominent lead melodies that sound wrong when suddenly muted.
  • Keep transients controlled so scene transitions feel smooth.
  • All stems in a pack should be the same length so loops align.

General Recording & Export Tips

  • Export clean loops with no clicks at boundaries.
  • Leave a little headroom — don't master too hot.
  • Avoid huge dynamic swings inside one file.
  • Keep each file focused on one role.
  • Shorter, clean loops often work better than long, complex recordings.
Importing in the app Go to Settings → Engine Sound → Import Audio (for RPM packs) or Import Stems (for Sound Drive packs). Select individual WAV/MP3 files or a single ZIP archive containing them. A single file creates a one-sample RPM pack. Multiple files with the same prefix create either a classic RPM pack or a Sound Drive pack automatically based on the naming pattern. A single import cannot mix RPM names and Sound Drive stem names. After importing a Sound Drive pack, use Edit Scenes to configure arrangements and triggers.
Missing files are safe If a previously imported pack is deleted or missing, the app falls back to automatic selection instead of crashing. No manual cleanup required.
